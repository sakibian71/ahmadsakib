Differential equation is a mathematical equation that relates a function with its derivatives. It describes how a quantity changes in relation to another, often representing physical phenomena such as motion, growth, or decay. Differential equations can be classified as ordinary (ODEs) or partial (PDEs) depending on whether they involve derivatives with respect to one variable or multiple variables.

Newton’s Law of Cooling – Coffee Problem(9.1, E14)

Suppose you have just poured a cup of freshly brewed coffee with temperature in a room where the temperature is constant.

(a) When do you think the coffee cools most quickly? What happens to the rate of cooling as time goes by? Explain.

The coffee cools most quickly immediately after it is poured, when the temperature difference between the coffee and the room is the greatest. According to Newton’s Law of Cooling, the rate of cooling is proportional to this temperature difference.

As time goes by and the coffee cools, the temperature difference becomes smaller. As a result, the rate of cooling decreases gradually until the coffee reaches room temperature. At that point, cooling stops.

(b) Newton’s Law of Cooling Equation

Newton’s Law of Cooling states that the rate of change of temperature of an object is proportional to the difference between its current temperature and the ambient temperature, as long as this difference is not too large.

Let:

  • Tc(t)T_c(t)Tc​(t): temperature of the coffee at time ttt
  • TrT_rTr​: constant room temperature
  • k>0k > 0k>0: proportionality constant

The differential equation is:

dTcdt=−k(Tc−Tr)\frac{dT_c}{dt} = -k(T_c - T_r)dtdTc​​=−k(Tc​−Tr​)

Initial condition:
Tc(0)=T0T_c(0) = T_0Tc​(0)=T0​
where T0T_0T0​ is the initial temperature of the coffee.

This differential equation is appropriate for modeling the cooling of coffee, especially when the temperature difference is moderate and the surrounding conditions (room temperature) are constant.

(c) Sketch of the Solution

The solution to the differential equation is:

Tc(t)=Tr+(T0−Tr)e−ktT_c(t) = T_r + (T_0 - T_r)e^{-kt}Tc​(t)=Tr​+(T0​−Tr​)e−kt

This represents an exponential decay curve:

  • The temperature starts at T0T_0T0​
  • It decreases rapidly at first
  • The curve gradually flattens and approaches TrT_rTr​ asymptotically
  • The coffee never actually "reaches" room temperature in finite time

We model the cooling of coffee using the formula:

T(t)=TR+(T0−TR)e−ktT(t) = T_R + (T_0 - T_R) e^{-kt}T(t)=TR​+(T0​−TR​)e−kt

Below is the Python code used to simulate and plot the cooling curve.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Defining constants
T_r = 20   # Room temperature
T_0 = 95   # Initial coffee temperature
k = 1      # Cooling constant

# Time array
t = np.linspace(0, 50, 1000)

# Temperature as a function of time using Newton's Law of Cooling
T_c = T_r + (T_0 - T_r) * np.exp(-k * t)

# Plotting temperature curve
plt.plot(t, T_c, label='Coffee Temperature')

# Adding a horizontal line for T_R
plt.axhline(y=T_r, color='red', linestyle='--', label='$T_R = 20^\circ$C')

# Plot formatting
plt.xlabel('Time (minutes)')
plt.ylabel('Temperature (°C)')
plt.title("Cooling of Coffee Over Time")
plt.grid(True)
plt.legend()
plt.show()
