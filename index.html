Simple Harmonic Oscillation

Simple Harmonic Oscillation (SHO) is defined as a type of motion where the restoring force is directly proportional to the displacement from the equilibrium position and acts in the opposite direction.

Mathematically, this is expressed as:

$$ F = -kx $$

Where:

  • $F$ is the restoring force
  • $k$ is the spring constant
  • $x$ is the displacement from equilibrium

The motion of a simple harmonic oscillator is periodic and can be described by the following equations:

$$ x(t) = A \cos(\omega t + \phi) $$

$$ v(t) = -A\omega \sin(\omega t + \phi) $$

$$ a(t) = -A\omega^2 \cos(\omega t + \phi) $$

Where:

  • $x(t)$ is the displacement at time $t$
  • $A$ is the amplitude
  • $\omega$ is the angular frequency, given by $\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T}$
  • $\phi$ is the phase constant
  • $v(t)$ is the velocity at time $t$
  • $a(t)$ is the acceleration at time $t$

The total mechanical energy of the system is conserved and given by:

$$ E = \frac{1}{2}kA^2 $$

Simple harmonic motion is a fundamental concept in physics and is observed in systems like springs, pendulums, and molecular vibrations.

Example

Consider a mass-spring system where a mass of 1 kg is attached to a spring with a spring constant of 100 N/m. If the mass is displaced by 0.1 m from its equilibrium position, the restoring force is:

$$ F = -kx = -(100)(0.1) = -10 \, \text{N} $$

The negative sign indicates that the force acts in the direction opposite to the displacement.

The angular frequency is:

$$ \omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{100}{1}} = 10 \, \text{rad/s} $$

The period of oscillation is:

$$ T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} = \frac{2\pi}{10} \approx 0.628 \, \text{s} $$

Therefore, the motion of the mass is described by the equations of simple harmonic motion, and it will oscillate back and forth around the equilibrium position with a period of approximately 0.628 seconds.
